Topic 01 of 08
The Transformer — The 2017 Revolution
The single paper that changed everything about AI forever.
The Architecture Behind Everything

Before 2017, AI struggled with language.

Every modern LLM — ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini — is built on one architecture invented in 2017: the Transformer. Understanding it means understanding why AI suddenly became so good at language.

Before the Transformer, AI used a model called RNN (Recurrent Neural Network) to process language. RNNs read text one word at a time, from left to right — like a person reading slowly, remembering each word before moving to the next.

This worked — but it had a critical weakness: by the time the RNN reached the end of a long sentence, it had "forgotten" the beginning. Like trying to remember the first word of a paragraph by the time you reach the last sentence.

🐢
RNN — Old Way (Before 2017)
Reads one word at a time → left to right.

"The cat that chased the mouse that lived in the house that Jack built sat on the ___"

By the time it reaches "___", it has forgotten "cat." It guesses wrong.
⚡
Transformer — New Way (2017+)
Reads ALL words simultaneously → sees the whole picture at once.

Immediately connects "sat" back to "cat" — no forgetting. Gets it right every time.

In June 2017, eight Google researchers published a paper titled "Attention Is All You Need." It introduced the Transformer — a new architecture that could process all words in a sentence at the same time, figuring out relationships between any two words regardless of distance.

The impact was immediate. Within two years, every major AI lab had abandoned RNNs and switched to Transformers. GPT, BERT, Claude, Gemini — all Transformers.

The Classroom Analogy
"RNN is like a student reading an essay by covering each word with their thumb, one at a time. Transformer is like a student who can see the entire essay at once — and immediately draw lines connecting related ideas anywhere on the page."
📌 The ability to see all words at once — and measure how strongly each word relates to every other word — is what makes Transformers so powerful at understanding language.
💡
Why "Attention Is All You Need"? The paper's title was a bold claim — that a simple mechanism called "attention" (no complex recurrence or convolution) was all you needed to achieve state-of-the-art language understanding. They were right. The paper has been cited over 100,000 times — one of the most influential in computing history.
Topic 02 of 08
The Attention Mechanism — How AI Understands Context
The single idea that makes language AI actually understand meaning.
The Core Innovation

Not all words matter equally.

Attention is the mechanism that lets the Transformer figure out which words in a sentence are most important to each other. It's why AI can understand ambiguous language — something that defeated every previous approach.

For every word in a sentence, the attention mechanism asks: "Which other words should I pay the most attention to when understanding this word?"

It assigns an "attention score" between every pair of words. High score = these words are closely related. Low score = these words barely affect each other. The model uses these scores to build a rich, context-aware understanding of every word.

Sentence: "The trophy didn't fit in the suitcase because it was too big."

This is what makes Transformers so revolutionary. Previous AI could not reliably resolve what "it" referred to in a complex sentence. The attention mechanism solves this by letting the model look across the entire sentence simultaneously and measure relationships.

🎯
Self-Attention
Words attend to other words in the same sentence. Used to understand meaning within one piece of text.
🔄
Multi-Head Attention
Multiple attention operations run in parallel — each "head" looks for different types of relationships. Grammar, meaning, coreference, all at once.
🌐
Cross-Attention
Words in one sequence attend to words in another. Used in translation — attending from English words to French words.
The Spotlight Analogy
"Attention is like a spotlight in a theatre. Instead of lighting the entire stage equally, the spotlight shines brightest on the most important actors for each scene. For each word, the Transformer spotlights the most relevant other words — dimming everything less important."
📌 GPT-4 runs 96 attention heads simultaneously — 96 different spotlights, each looking for different relationships, all at once.
Topic 03 of 08
Parameters & Weights — The AI Brain Size
The numbers that encode everything the AI has ever learned.
The Scale of Knowledge

Billions of tiny knobs, perfectly tuned.

Parameters are the numbers inside a neural network. They start random, get adjusted trillions of times during training, and by the end — they encode the patterns of all human language.

Imagine a mixing board in a recording studio — hundreds of sliders and knobs, each controlling one aspect of the sound. Now imagine a mixing board with 175 billion sliders — that's GPT-3. Each slider is a parameter, tuned during training to produce the correct output for any input.

Parameters are not "facts" stored in a database. They are patterns encoded as numbers. The knowledge that Paris is the capital of France isn't stored as text — it's distributed across millions of parameters that together make "Paris" the most likely output when asked about France's capital.

Topic 04 of 08
Embeddings — How Words Become Numbers
The bridge between human language and machine mathematics.
Words as Coordinates

AI doesn't see words. It sees vectors.

Before any processing can happen, words must be converted into numbers. Embeddings are that conversion — and they're smarter than you'd expect.

A computer can't process the word "cat" directly. It needs numbers. So each token gets converted into a long list of numbers called a vector or embedding. Think of it as GPS coordinates — but instead of locating a place on a map, it locates a word in "meaning space."

Words with similar meanings end up with similar number vectors — close together in meaning space. Words with very different meanings end up far apart.

Word Embeddings — Simplified Visualization
King
[0.82, 0.91, 0.14, 0.77, 0.33, ...]
Meaning space: royalty, power, male, human
Queen
[0.82, 0.90, 0.14, 0.22, 0.33, ...]
Very similar to King — only the "gender" dimension differs
Apple
[0.11, 0.03, 0.88, 0.04, 0.91, ...]
Completely different — fruit, food, object, no royalty connection
Chennai
[0.55, 0.21, 0.44, 0.67, 0.12, ...]
Close to Mumbai, Delhi — city, India, location, culture
🔢 Real embeddings have thousands of dimensions — not just 5. GPT-4 uses 12,288-dimensional vectors. Each dimension encodes a different aspect of meaning: gender, animacy, location, sentiment, formality, and thousands more.

The most famous example of embedding arithmetic:

# Famous embedding arithmetic that proves AI understands meaning

King − Man + Woman = Queen   ✅

Paris − France + India = Delhi   ✅

Walking − Walk + Run = Running   ✅

# This works because the vector arithmetic captures semantic relationships
# "King - Man" = the concept of royalty without gender
# Add "Woman" = female royalty = Queen
The City Map Analogy
"Embeddings are like placing words on a map. Similar words live in the same neighbourhood — 'happy', 'joyful', 'elated' are all nearby. 'Sad' is across town. 'Car' is in a completely different city. The AI navigates this map to understand meaning."
📌 When you type a prompt, every word gets converted to its embedding coordinates. The Transformer then reasons about the relationships between all these coordinates to generate a response.
Topic 05 of 08
Temperature — Creative vs Safe AI
The dial that controls how adventurous or conservative the AI is.
Controlling Randomness

Same prompt. Different answers. Why?

Ask ChatGPT the same question twice and you get different answers. This isn't a bug — it's temperature at work. Temperature controls how random or predictable the AI's responses are.

When an LLM generates the next token, it produces a probability score for every possible token. For example, after "The sky is", it might say: "blue" = 70%, "dark" = 15%, "clear" = 10%, "purple" = 4%, "green" = 1%...

Temperature controls how the AI chooses from these probabilities. Low temperature = always picks the highest probability. High temperature = more willing to pick less likely options.

Topic 07 of 08
LLM Limitations — Where They Break Down
The four critical weaknesses every AI user must understand.
The Hard Limits

Powerful — but not infallible.

LLMs have real, serious limitations that cause real problems in the real world. Understanding these is just as important as understanding their capabilities.

🌀
Hallucination
Confidently stating things that are completely false
MOST DANGEROUS

Hallucination is the single most dangerous flaw in LLMs. Because LLMs generate responses by predicting what text should look like — not by retrieving verified facts — they sometimes generate text that sounds completely real but is entirely made up.

The AI doesn't know it's hallucinating. It has no "uncertainty alarm." It states false information with the same confident tone as true information. This is what makes it dangerous — especially in medical, legal, or financial contexts.

Real Example of Hallucination:
Ask an LLM: "Tell me about the Tamil poet Murugesan who wrote Kavitha Kalanjiyam in 1987."
The LLM might respond: "Murugesan was a celebrated Tamil poet born in Madurai in 1952. Kavitha Kalanjiyam, published in 1987, received the Sahitya Akademi award in 1989..."
This person and book may not exist at all. The LLM generated a plausible-sounding answer.
How to protect yourself: Always verify factual claims from LLMs using authoritative sources. Never use LLM output as the sole source for medical, legal, or financial decisions. If an answer can be verified — verify it.
📅
Knowledge Cutoff
The AI is frozen in time — it knows nothing after its training date
SIGNIFICANT

LLMs are trained on data up to a specific date and then frozen. After that cutoff, the world moves on — but the AI doesn't. It has no awareness of events, discoveries, elections, or releases that happened after training.

ModelKnowledge CutoffWhat It Doesn't Know
GPT-3.5September 2021Everything after — 3+ years of world events
GPT-4April 2023Recent AI developments, elections, discoveries
Claude 3.5Early 2024Latest months of news and releases
Gemini 1.5Early 2024Recent global events
The solution: Tools like Perplexity AI and ChatGPT with Browse add real-time web search on top of LLMs. The LLM handles reasoning; the search tool provides current information. Together they solve the cutoff problem.
📝
Context Window Limit
The AI can only "see" a limited amount of text at once
PRACTICAL

Every LLM has a fixed context window — the maximum amount of text it can process at one time. Think of it as the AI's working desk. Once the desk is full, new papers push old ones off the edge — the AI forgets the beginning of the conversation.

This means very long conversations, large documents, or extensive code bases may exceed the context window, causing the AI to lose track of earlier important information.

Real scenario: You paste a 500-page legal contract into Claude and ask it to find clauses about liability. If the contract exceeds 200K tokens, the AI cannot process the full document in one go. Solution: chunk the document into sections and process each separately — or use models with larger context windows.
⚖️
Bias & Fairness
The AI reflects the biases in its training data
SYSTEMIC

LLMs learn from internet text — and internet text reflects society's biases. If medical articles primarily show male doctors and female nurses, the AI absorbs that pattern. If most CEO articles use "he," the AI may do the same. These biases are often subtle and hard to detect.

Types of bias found in LLMs include: gender bias (associating roles with genders), cultural bias (Western perspectives dominating), racial bias (harmful stereotypes from training data), and recency bias (recent internet trends over-represented).

Active research area: Every major AI company has dedicated teams working on bias detection and mitigation. It's one of the hardest unsolved problems in AI — because biases in data are often reflections of real-world inequalities, not just errors.
Topic 08 of 08
The Prompt Journey — What Happens in 1 Second
The complete technical journey from your keypress to the AI's response.
End to End

You press Enter. Here is everything that happens.

In approximately one second, your text travels through eight distinct technical stages before the first word of a response appears. Here is every step.

YOUR PROMPT: "Explain photosynthesis like I am 10 years old"

STEP 1:  Tokenize  → ["Explain", "photo", "syn", "thesis", "like", "I", "am", "10", ...]
STEP 2:  Embed     → Each token → 12,288 numbers (vector)
STEP 3:  Stack     → All token vectors → input matrix
STEP 4:  Attention → 96 heads compute relationships simultaneously
STEP 5:  96 Transformer layers refine understanding
STEP 6:  Output layer → probability scores for all 50,000 tokens
STEP 7:  Temperature sampling → picks next token
STEP 8:  Detokenize → number → word → streamed to your screen

OUTPUT (word 1): "Photosynthesis"
→ Repeat steps 4-8 for EVERY subsequent word until response is complete
01
TOKENIZATION
Your text is broken into tokens
Your message is split into tokens — chunks of roughly 3–4 characters. "Photosynthesis" becomes multiple tokens. Spaces, punctuation, and special characters all become their own tokens. The full prompt becomes a sequence of token IDs (numbers).
02
EMBEDDING
Each token becomes a high-dimensional vector
Every token ID is converted into a vector — a list of thousands of numbers representing the word's meaning. "Explain" gets a vector encoding: verb, imperative, clarification request. "10 years old" gets a vector encoding: young, child, simplify. These vectors carry semantic meaning into the model.
03
POSITIONAL ENCODING
Position information is added
Since the Transformer processes all tokens simultaneously, it needs to know their order. Positional encoding adds information about where each token sits in the sequence — token 1, token 2, token 3. Without this, "dog bites man" and "man bites dog" would look identical to the model.
04
MULTI-HEAD ATTENTION
96 attention heads analyse relationships simultaneously
Every token attends to every other token. 96 attention heads run in parallel — each looking for different types of relationships. One head might focus on grammatical relationships. Another on semantic similarity. Another on coreference (what "it" refers to). All 96 results are combined into a rich contextual representation.
05
96 TRANSFORMER LAYERS
Deep understanding builds layer by layer
The output of attention flows through 96 sequential transformer layers. Each layer further refines the representation — early layers handle surface features (word types, grammar), middle layers handle semantic meaning, deep layers handle abstract reasoning and intent. By layer 96, the model has a rich understanding of your prompt's full meaning.
06
TOKEN PREDICTION
Probability scores for every possible next token
The final layer outputs a probability score for every token in the vocabulary (~50,000 tokens). "Photosynthesis" = 42%, "Plants" = 18%, "This" = 12%... The model hasn't chosen yet — it has a full distribution of possibilities.
07
TEMPERATURE SAMPLING
Temperature adjusts probabilities, one token is selected
Temperature reshapes the probability distribution — flattening it (more randomness) or sharpening it (more determinism). Then one token is sampled from the adjusted distribution. At temperature 0.7, "Photosynthesis" wins. The token is converted back to text and immediately streamed to your screen.
08
REPEAT
The entire process repeats for every single word
The selected token is appended to the prompt, and steps 1–7 run again to generate the next token. A 200-word response requires approximately 270 separate prediction cycles — each one processing the entire conversation history. That's why responses appear word by word, streaming in real time.

Q 01 / 15Basic
What was the name of the 2017 paper that introduced the Transformer architecture?
✅ "Attention Is All You Need" — published by eight Google researchers in June 2017. It introduced the Transformer architecture and the self-attention mechanism. It has been cited over 100,000 times and is considered one of the most influential papers in computing history.
Q 02 / 15Basic
What was the key weakness of RNNs that Transformers solved?
✅ The forgetting problem — RNNs processed text left to right, one word at a time. By the end of a long sentence, the beginning was effectively "forgotten." Transformers process all tokens simultaneously, so every word can directly attend to every other word regardless of distance.
Q 03 / 15⚡ Tricky
In the sentence "The trophy didn't fit in the suitcase because it was too big" — what does "it" refer to, and how does the Transformer figure it out?
✅ The trophy. The attention mechanism connects "it" → "big" → "trophy" (the big thing that didn't fit). It also has a high attention score between "fit" and "suitcase" — understanding that the suitcase was the container, not the big thing. This is called coreference resolution — one of the hardest problems in NLP that Transformers finally solved reliably.
Q 04 / 15Basic
What is a word embedding?
✅ A vector representing meaning. Each word/token is converted to a list of thousands of numbers. Words with similar meanings get similar vectors — they are "close" in the high-dimensional space. This allows mathematical operations on meaning: King − Man + Woman = Queen works because the vectors encode semantic relationships.
Q 05 / 15⚡ Tricky
King − Man + Woman = Queen. This works with embeddings. What does this prove?
✅ It proves semantic structure is captured mathematically. The vector difference "King − Man" captures the concept of royalty without gender. Adding "Woman" adds the female gender direction — landing on "Queen." This is mathematical, not conscious. The AI doesn't "understand" royalty — it has learned that these words relate in this geometric way from training data.
Q 06 / 15Basic
What does a high temperature setting (e.g. 1.5) do to an LLM's output?
✅ More randomness = more creativity. High temperature flattens the probability distribution — the AI considers lower-probability tokens more seriously. Great for creative writing and brainstorming. Bad for factual accuracy — the AI might pick a "creative" but wrong answer. Temperature 0 = always the safest choice.
Q 07 / 15⚡ Tricky
You ask Claude at temperature 0: "What is 2 + 2?" You ask again five times. What happens?
✅ Temperature 0 = deterministic. At temperature 0, the model always picks the single highest-probability token. For "2+2=", "4" has overwhelming probability. Every run produces identical output. This is why temperature 0 is used for code generation and mathematical tasks — you need reproducible, correct answers.
Q 08 / 15Basic
What is AI hallucination?
✅ Confident false information. LLMs generate text by predicting what a real answer would look like — not by verifying facts. Sometimes the most statistically likely next words form a plausible-sounding but entirely false statement. The AI doesn't know it's wrong. It never has an "uncertainty alarm" — it generates with equal confidence whether correct or hallucinating.
Q 09 / 15⚡ Tricky
A doctor uses an LLM to look up a drug dosage. The AI confidently gives a wrong dose. Who is responsible?
✅ The doctor bears professional responsibility. All major LLMs include disclaimers that they should not be used as sole sources for medical, legal, or financial decisions. LLMs hallucinate — this is a known limitation. Professional responsibility requires verifying critical information from authoritative sources. AI is a tool, not an authority.
Q 10 / 15Basic
What does the knowledge cutoff mean for an LLM?
✅ The frozen date. After the cutoff, the world moves on but the AI doesn't. Ask an LLM with a 2023 cutoff about a 2024 election — it genuinely doesn't know. It might hallucinate an answer based on patterns, which is even more dangerous. Always check an AI's knowledge cutoff when asking about recent events.
Q 11 / 15⚡ Tricky
Why does an LLM generate text word by word (streaming) rather than all at once?
✅ Each token requires the previous tokens. The model generates token 1, then uses the full prompt + token 1 to generate token 2, then uses all of that to generate token 3 — and so on. It's genuinely sequential — each word depends on everything before it. Streaming to screen is not a delay trick — it reflects the actual generation process.
Q 12 / 15Basic
What is positional encoding in a Transformer?
✅ Word order information. Since Transformers process all tokens simultaneously (not left-to-right like RNNs), they would otherwise have no idea which word came first. Positional encoding adds a unique numerical signal to each token's embedding based on its position — token 1 gets signal A, token 2 gets signal B — so the model knows the sequence order.
Q 13 / 15⚡ Tricky
An LLM trained mostly on English internet data will likely exhibit which type of bias?
✅ Cultural and linguistic bias. The internet skews heavily toward English-language, Western content. An LLM trained on this data will have richer, more accurate knowledge of Western concepts, history, and culture compared to Tamil, African, or Asian contexts. This is why AI performs better in English than Tamil — not because Tamil is harder, but because it has less representation in training data.
Q 14 / 15⚡ Tricky
Multi-head attention runs multiple attention operations in parallel. Why is this better than single attention?
✅ Different heads capture different relationship types. Imagine reading a sentence looking only for grammatical structure (one head), then reading again looking only for who-refers-to-what (another head), then again for topic relationships (another head). You'd understand the sentence far better than any single reading. That's what 96 attention heads do simultaneously — building a multi-dimensional understanding of every token relationship.
Q 15 / 15⚡ Tricky — Final Boss
A student says: "If I keep the context window full with our conversation, the AI remembers everything we've ever discussed." Is this correct?
✅ The context window is NOT permanent memory. It's a temporary working desk. Start a new conversation — the desk is cleared completely. Even within a conversation, if you exceed the context limit, the oldest messages slide off the edge and are forgotten. True persistent memory requires external storage (databases, retrieval systems) added on top of the LLM — which is what AI Agents use, covered in Day 7.
